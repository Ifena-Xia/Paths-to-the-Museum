Paths to the Museum: Parental Education and Children’s Cultural Participation in China

Author: Yifang Xia

1. Why Museum Participation Matters?

Museum participation is not simply a matter of leisure—it is a form of learning, cultural identification, and long-term civic development. Sandell & Zimmerman (2017), Lin et al. (2023) highlight the potential of museum environments to shape reflective teaching and interactive learning. Holmes (2011), Acosta & Haden (2022) show that even brief museum-based tinkering experiences can generate gains in science learning and reflective thinking, particularly when scaffolded by facilitation and parents’ own education. Early museum visits foster children’s cognitive, creative, and social capacities (Cronin et al., 2020), and support learning transfer into the home environment, where caregivers mediate and extend the experience. Temple and Gan (2023) demonstrate that early involvement in cultural activities significantly predicts lifelong engagement, especially among men. Paredes (2016) illustrates immigrant identity may inspire engagement with museums as spaces for cultural validation and civic inclusion. In this way, museums act not only as educational tools but as vehicles of intergenerational transmission.

More broadly, participation in cultural life carries long-term implications for wellbeing. Løkken et al. (2020) find that cultural engagement, including receptive activities like museum visits, is associated with reduced all-cause mortality.

2. What shapes participation—and what restricts it?

Despite their growing visibility as educational environments, museums remain unequally accessed and unevenly experienced. Across contexts, one predictor stands out: education, especially parental education, emerges as the most consistent determinant of cultural participation (Falk & Katz-Gerro, 2016; Zieba, 2017). Tertiary education increases both the probability and frequency of attendance (Muñiz et al., 2017; Suarez-Fernandez et al., 2020; Bone et al., 2021), often surpassing income or occupational class in explanatory power (Haag & Specht, 2022; Fiorillo & Ofria, 2024). Cultural engagement is shaped not only by access to resources, but by the ability to navigate and decode highbrow spaces (Brida et al., 2016). This cultural capital, deeply embedded in class-based educational trajectories, is transmitted through families over time (Váradi & Józsa, 2023; Crispin & Beck, 2025).

Studies have extended this insight to children’s participation in cultural life. Mak and Fancourt (2021) show that out-of-school participation in arts and cultural activities is strongly predicted by parents’ own cultural engagement, neighborhood deprivation, and socioeconomic status. While school-based programs may flatten inequality temporarily, family influence remains the dominant force in long-term participation (Temple & Gan, 2023). Borrione et al. (2021) further observe that frequent-attending families tend to value museum staff expertise, educational content, and learning outcomes, while infrequent-attending families prioritize rest areas, multimedia, and play, suggesting that class-based orientations influence not just whether, but how families engage with museums.

Geography compounds these divides. Spatial barriers, including rural residence, neighborhood deprivation, and infrastructure scarcity, affect both access and perceived cultural belonging (Mak et al., 2021; Rodríguez-Puello & Iturra, 2024). Intra-household studies like Ateca-Amestoy and Ugidos (2021) reveal that women’s participation in cultural life is further shaped by childcare responsibilities and mobility constraints, suggesting that structural inequalities—not just preferences—govern family engagement with museums. Olivares and Piatak (2022) add a racial lens, showing that Black Americans are significantly less likely to attend museums than white peers, even after controlling for education and income. Yet Latinx and Black respondents are more likely to cite cultural heritage as a motivation for attendance, emphasizing the importance of representation and institutional culture.

At the macro level, Ateca-Amestoy & Prieto-Rodriguez (2024) confirm that education explains more inequality in cultural participation than race or geography. Importantly, these inequalities are mirrored in digital spaces, undermining the idea that online access resolves exclusion. Instead, cultural engagement, whether live or digital, remains clustered among the already-engaged.

3. The Case of China: rapid growth, persistent inequality

Despite rapid museum development in China, access remains unequal. Free admission doesn’t guarantee inclusion: digital booking, lack of signage, or limited programs still exclude disadvantaged groups. Using CEPS data, this study explores how parental education shapes children’s museum participation across urban and rural regions.

Data Processing and Analysis

The study draws on CEPS data from 19,487 families. After cleaning, 9,761 records with full responses were analyzed. Variables include participation frequency, parental education, gender, grade, siblings, living arrangement, and urbanicity level.

This study uses data from the China Education Panel Survey (CEPS), which began in the 2013–2014 academic year with two baseline cohorts: 7th grade (the first year of junior high school) and 9th grade (the third year). Based on stratified sampling that accounted for factors such as the average education level of the population and the proportion of the floating population, CEPS selected 28 county-level units (counties, districts, or cities) across China as survey sites. Within these sites, 112 schools and 438 classes were randomly selected, and all students in those classes were surveyed, resulting in data from 19,487 entries.

To examine the relationship between students’ cultural participation and parental education, I use several variables drawn from both student and parent questionnaires:

1. Cultural participation frequency, measured by parental responses to the item “Visiting museums, zoos, science museums, etc.” in response to the question “How often did family members do the following together with this child over the past year?”

2. Student gender, parental education level, grade, and number of siblings, reported by students;

3. Residential area, reported by parents;

4. Living arrangements (with whom is the surveyed child living with), reported by both students and parents.

Due to China’s hukou (household registration) system, it is common for children to remain in their registered hometowns while their parents migrate to urban areas for work. In such contexts, there may be inconsistencies between the number of siblings currently living with the surveyed child and the total number of siblings in the family. However, because this left-behind child situation is underreported—only 3,299 families formally noted it in the survey—and cannot be comprehensively captured, I use the total number of siblings reported by students, regardless of co-residence.

After removing cases with missing data, the dataset includes 18,292 valid student reports and 17,513 valid parent reports. To ensure data reliability, I compare the household composition reported by students and parents, and retain only those families with matching reports. After cleaning and merging the relevant variables, the analytic sample includes 9,789 families with complete and internally consistent information. Among them 28 records are further removed since they don’t give clear indications about the living areas, ending up with 9761 records for regression. Among all these records, 52.2% are females while 47.8% are males, 51.5% are grade 7 while 48.5% are grade 9. OLS Model

To prepare the dataset for ordinary least squares (OLS) regression, I transformed both the dependent and key independent variables into numeric formats. The dependent variable, cultural participation frequency, was recoded from six ordered categories (“Never” to “More than once a week”) into an ordinal numeric scale ranging from 1 to 6.

For the primary independent variable, parental education, I created a new variable parent_edu by selecting the higher educational attainment between the mother and father. Educational qualifications were converted into years of schooling. Although Technical Secondary School, Vocational High School, and Senior High School differ in curriculum — emphasizing manual skills, applied theory, and academic preparation for Gaokao respectively — all were assigned 12 years, reflecting their standard program lengths. Similarly, I standardized junior college at 15 years (9 years basic + 3 years high school + 3 years college), bachelor’s degrees at 16 years, and master’s degrees at 19 years (assuming three years post-undergraduate), acknowledging that program durations may vary but choosing the most typical paths. The mean of parents’ years of schooling was 11.07 (SD = 3.11). Bar charts and box plots illustrate the distributions of participation frequency and parental education under these continuous assumptions.

Several categorical variables were recoded into binary dummy variables to facilitate regression analysis. Gender was coded as female (1 = female, 0 = male). Grade was captured by grade9 (1 = ninth grade, 0 = seventh grade). To better reflect the gradient of urbanicity in the sample, I recoded the original living area categories into a new variable, urbanicity_level, with three levels: (1) Urban core (36.3%), including respondents living in the central area of a city or county; (2) Semi-urban (28.1%), combining the outskirts of cities/counties, rural-urban continuum areas, and towns outside city boundaries; and (3) Rural (35.6%), representing those living in designated rural areas. As stated above, the small number of observations categorized as “Other” were excluded from this classification due to ambiguity. This categorization allows us to examine how varying degrees of urbanicity relate to other social or behavioral outcomes. To account for family size, I coded has_siblings as 1 for students with one or more siblings, which includes 49.7% of the sample, and 0 for students with no siblings (50.3%). For household composition, I created standard_living, coded as 1 for students who live either with both their mother and father or with mother, father, and grandparents — a grouping that comprises 48% of the sample — and 0 for all other combinations of cohabitation.

Below is the resulting OLS Model 1 for estimated participation frequency:

frequency = 1.784 + 0.0578 parent_edu - 0.1233 semi_urban - 0.4987 rural + 0.0556 female - 0.1863 grade9 - 0.1222 has_siblings + 0.0622 standard_living

Given previous studies suggesting that the relationship between parental education and participation may vary by region, I introduced interaction terms between parental education and urbanicity levels. Model 2 estimates:

frequency = 2.006 + 0.0401 parent_edu - 0.4773 semi_urban - 0.9231 rural + 0.0293 parent_edu_semi_urban + 0.0391 parent_edu_rural + 0.0561 female - 0.1813 grade9 - 0.1251 has_siblings + 0.0655 standard_living

An F-test comparing the two models yielded a p-value of 0.0006, indicating that the interaction terms significantly improve model fit. Thus, Model 2 is preferred. From Model 2, we observe that participation frequency decreases notably as urbanicity declines. However, higher parental education partially offsets this regional disadvantage: its positive influence on participation becomes stronger in lower-urbanicity areas. Additionally, female students, 7th graders, only children, and those in standard family structures are predicted to have higher participation frequencies.

Binary Logistic Model

For binary logistic regression, I recoded the dependent variable, cultural participation frequency, into a binary outcome: responses of “Never” and “Once a year,” occupying 67.5%, were coded as 0 (none or low participation), and all other frequencies were coded as 1 (higher participation).

Parental education was categorized into three levels: (1) Low – junior high school and below (≤9 years), (2) Medium – technical secondary school, vocational high school, and senior high school (≈12 years), and (3) High – junior college and above (≥15 years). I assigned the higher of the two parents’ education levels as the final variable parent_edu, resulting in three dummy-coded categories: parent_edu_l, parent_edu_m, and parent_edu_h. This led to 4902 (50.2%), 2770 (28.3%), and 2089 (21.4%) falling into low, middle, high categories. The bar chart below presents the respective proportions of none or low participation and higher participation in these three parental education levels.

Additional covariates included: gender (female, 1 = female, 0 = male), grade level (grade9, 1 = 9th grade, 0 = 7th grade), family size (has_siblings, 1 = has one or more siblings, 0 = no siblings), and living areas (urbanicity_level, including urban core, semi-urban, and rural). Household composition was captured by standard_living, coded as 1 for students living with both mother and father (with or without grandparents), and 0 for all other combinations.

Binary logistic regressions were run with and without interaction terms between parental education and urbanicity. Based on BIC, the model without interactions fits better (the BIC of the model without interactions is 11574.48 while that with interactions is 11603.5).

Reflection on Interaction Terms

A particularly interesting finding is the disappearance of the interaction between parental education and living area after cleaning and recoding. Before adjusting for family composition (which resulted in the loss of ~10,000 cases) and collapsing parental education into three categories, the interaction between rural status and parental education remained salient across models, including both OLS and different logit regressions. After cleaning, the rural proportion dropped from 41% to 35.6%, and the categorical collapse of educational levels reduced variability. Both factors likely contributed to the loss of statistical significance for the interaction. This reflects how data cleaning and variable transformation can sometimes obscure meaningful relational structures in social data.

Conclusion

This study examines how parental education and geographic location shape children’s cultural participation in China, using survey data from CEPS. Consistent with previous research, parental education and living area emerge as most cogent predictors of participation across all models. Higher educational attainment among parents and urban residence significantly increase children’s frequency of museum and cultural visits, even after controlling for gender, grade, and household structure.

Importantly, interaction terms between parental education and urbanicity, initially salient, disappeared after dataset cleaning and variable categorization. This suggests that reduced rural representation and the collapse of educational levels into broad categories may obscure subtle correlation between parental education and regions.

The biggest limitation of this study is failing to consider museum visits organized by schools.

Overall, we conclude that despite policy efforts to democratize museum access, cultural participation remains closely tied to family background and spatial advantage. As museums continue to expand as sites of learning and identity-building, addressing these underlying disparities—particularly through more inclusive design and outreach—is critical to ensuring equitable access. Besides eliminating admission fees, other measures such as reducing course load, supporting children from single-parent families or children brought up by relatives other than parents, financing cultural programs in disadvantaged areas need to be taken.
